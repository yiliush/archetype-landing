The bottleneck

Anyone can spawn fifty agents. Nobody can manage them.

Coordination, not compute, is the wall. The interfaces we have are flat — chat tabs, terminal lists, PR queues — and flat structures cap out at the five-to-seven contexts a human can actually hold. GitHub's own framing for its multi-agent prototype admits it: "one developer, two dozen agents, zero alignment."

Flat tools
  • ✗ N agents = N interleaved transcripts to read
  • ✗ Every session needs an invented name
  • ✗ Recency-sorted lists destroy meaning
  • ✗ Relations between agents' work are unrepresentable
  • ✗ Transcripts report what the agent said, not what the project is

The mechanism

Placement, not reporting.

The document tree is your externalized mental model — you built it, so its structure is already in your head. Spawning an agent at a place means the geometry does the remembering.

1

Legibility breaks before control does

Scaling agents isn't a control problem, it's a reading problem. One human loses the ability to understand what all her agents are doing long before she loses the ability to stop them. Attention cost in flat tools is O(N).

2

Hierarchy is the ancient fix — the doc tree is its friendliest form

Org charts, filesystems, Wikipedia: every structure humans use to scale coordination is a tree with scoped responsibility. Situating an agent scopes what it knows (read up), who it knows (siblings), and what it may touch (write down). Lexical scoping — for labor.

3

You place agents; you don't brief them

The place remembers what it's doing and how its scope relates to everything else — as visible geometry (under this section, next to that agent), not facts you hold in your head. A memory palace you were already building anyway.

4

Agents write their work out at their position

Placement is the index; the agent writing back where it sits is the report. Together they collapse "what are my agents doing?" into "what's changed in my project?" — a question humans already answer at scale by reading, folding, and zooming a tree.

The payoff

Attention scales with the tree, not the agent count.

And because humans and agents coordinate through the same substrate, more humans scale the same way. Wikipedia is the existence proof.

The big vision doesn't land as hype — it lands as relief. Relief from the inbox of terminal tabs you're afraid of. You spin up the fifty-first agent the way you'd add a bullet to an outline: you put it where it belongs, fold it away, and trust that the place will tell you what it's doing when you come back to read.

Start with one page and one terminal. The curve is stated; the first point is honest.
